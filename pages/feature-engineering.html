Bag of Words (BoW)

A text representation that models a document as an unordered collection of word counts, disregarding grammar and word order.

Converts variable-length text into fixed-length numerical vectors for machine learning classifiers.

Constructs a vocabulary from the entire corpus, then counts each word's frequency per document, creating a sparse matrix of size documents × vocabulary.

Doc1: "मी शाळेत जातो"
→ {मी:1, शाळेत:1, जातो:1}
Doc2: "ती शाळेत जाते"
→ {ती:1, शाळेत:1, जाते:1}

Advantages
  • Simple to implement & understand
  • Works well for classification
  • Fast training and inference
Limitations
  • Ignores word order & context
  • High-dimensional sparse vectors
  • No handling of OOV words

N-Grams

A contiguous sequence of n items (words or characters) extracted from text, preserving local word order information.

Captures short-range contextual patterns and common phrases that individual words miss.

A sliding window of size n moves across the text; each unique n-gram becomes a feature with its frequency as the value.

"मी शाळेत जातो"
Bigrams (n=2): ["मी शाळेत", "शाळेत जातो"]
Trigrams (n=3): ["मी शाळेत जातो"]

Advantages
  • Preserves local word order
  • Captures common collocations
  • Improves over BoW accuracy
Limitations
  • Exponential feature explosion
  • Still sparse for large n
  • No long-range dependencies

TF (Term Frequency)

A measure of how frequently a term appears in a document, normalized by document length.

Quantifies the importance of a term within a specific document while accounting for document length variation.

TF(t,d) = count(t in d) / total terms in d. Common variants include log normalization (1 + log(count)) and augmented frequency (0.5 + 0.5 × TF).

"मराठी भाषा मराठी संस्कृती"
TF("मराठी") = 2/4 = 0.5
TF("भाषा") = 1/4 = 0.25

Advantages
  • Length normalization enables fairness
  • Simple computation
  • Interpretable scores
Limitations
  • Common words dominate
  • No cross-document discrimination
  • Length bias persists

IDF (Inverse Document Frequency)

A measure of how rare or unique a term is across the entire corpus, computed as the log of the inverse document frequency.

Down-weights common terms appearing in many documents and up-weights rare terms that are more discriminative.

IDF(t) = log(N / df(t)) where N is total documents and df(t) is the number of documents containing term t. Rare terms get higher IDF scores.

"महाराष्ट्र" in 5/100 docs
→ IDF = log(100/5) = 1.30
"आहे" in 95/100 docs
→ IDF = log(100/95) = 0.02

Advantages
  • Reduces stopword impact
  • Highlights discriminative terms
  • Language-agnostic computation
Limitations
  • Over-emphasizes rare/typo terms
  • Assumes power-law distribution
  • Sensitive to corpus composition

TF-IDF

The product of Term Frequency and Inverse Document Frequency, producing a weight that reflects both local importance and global rarity.

Identifies terms that are frequent within a document but rare across the corpus — the most distinctive features for classification and retrieval.

TF-IDF(t,d) = TF(t,d) × IDF(t). High weight when a term appears many times in few documents; low weight when it appears in nearly all documents.

"न्यायालय" (court) in doc: TF=0.1, IDF=2.0
→ TF-IDF = 0.20
"आहे" (is) in doc: TF=0.05, IDF=0.02
→ TF-IDF = 0.001

Advantages
  • Balances local & global importance
  • Highly interpretable
  • Reduces stopword influence
Limitations
  • Still bag-of-words (no order)
  • No semantics / synonymy
  • Static, non-contextual weights
